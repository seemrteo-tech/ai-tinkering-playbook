Simple definition

AI is when a computer system can do tasks that usually need human thinking ‚Äî like recognising patterns, understanding language, or making predictions.

In one sentence

AI learns from data to make useful decisions or outputs (with limits and mistakes).

Teacher anchor: AI is not ‚Äúmagic.‚Äù It is pattern-finding + rules + probability ‚Äî working on lots of examples.

What AI can do (classroom examples)

  • Sort photos (cats vs dogs) by learning visual patterns.
  • Suggest the next word in a sentence (predictive text).
  • Turn speech into text (captions) by matching sound patterns.
  • Spot trends in numbers (e.g., attendance patterns).

What AI cannot do (common myths)

  • It does not ‚Äúunderstand‚Äù like a human (it predicts patterns).
  • It is not always correct ‚Äî it can hallucinate/guess.
  • It can inherit bias from the data it learns from.
  • It needs clear goals and checking by humans.

What is AI tinkering?

AI tinkering is hands-on exploration of AI tools and ideas ‚Äî testing, observing, adjusting, and reflecting ‚Äî to learn how AI behaves.

Why it matters for teachers

It builds confidence to use AI wisely: you learn what to trust, what to verify, and how to teach with guardrails.

Mindset: Tinkering is not about being an AI expert ‚Äî it‚Äôs about being a curious tester.

Core moves in AI tinkering

  • Ask: What is the goal? What would success look like?
  • Try: Use prompts/inputs and run a few tests.
  • Compare: What changes when the input changes?
  • Evaluate: Is it accurate, fair, safe, age-appropriate?
  • Improve: Adjust constraints, examples, and instructions.
  • Reflect: What did the tool do well/poorly? When would you use it?

Safe use reminders (quick)

  • Do not paste student full names, NRIC, addresses, or private data into tools.
  • Verify outputs (facts, sources, maths, tone) before sharing with students.
  • Keep prompts specific: audience, level, length, success criteria.
  • Model ‚ÄúAI as a draft helper,‚Äù not ‚ÄúAI as the answer key.‚Äù
